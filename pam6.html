Stop 6

The final Pam the bird sighting of our tour, painted across the roof adjacent to the historic John Darling & Son flour mill, embodies a powerful meeting of Melbourne’s industrial past and its contemporary street art culture. The factory, once a symbol of local production and labour, now serves as a canvas for creative reclamation. Seen from the train, Pam’s sweeping form invites recognition among commuters as a part of the ongoing “spot the bird” phenomenon that turns ordinary travel into an act of engagement with place. Its placement on an elevated, inaccessible surface mirrors the shifting boundaries between public and private space in the post-industrial era.

Pam’s presence also embodies the inherent tension of graffiti. As Dovey et al., (2012) argue, graffiti embodies tension between art and transgression; vandalism and art occupy overlapping spaces of creative defiance. Positioned on industrial walls, it injects vitality into neglected spaces while retaining an edge of illegality, reflecting Melbourne’s street art culture. This dual role highlights graffiti’s potential to produce social and spatial value, moving beyond aesthetic enhancement to generate engagement and delight within public spaces.

Across each stop, Pam the Bird emerges as a potent symbol of how tactical creativity reshapes the everyday city. From the precarious fence-line works near Flinders Street Station to the monumental silos of the western suburbs, Pam continually negotiates visibility, danger, and belonging.

In this sense, Pam is not simply an image but a spatial practice: it enlivens forgotten structures, activates the commuter’s gaze, and reclaims infrastructures of transit and production as spaces of cultural meaning.
