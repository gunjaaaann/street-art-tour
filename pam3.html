Stop 3

This iteration of Pam, tucked near Fitzroy station assumes a more elusive presence than some of her more prominent counterparts. From the train, the viewer only encounters smaller, less colourful Pams along this wall. Their subtle presence encourages a closer inspection, prompting a hop off the train to photograph them, leading to this larger, discreet iteration, highlighting how street art invites active engagement and serendipitous encounters. As Beattie (2020) argues, “artworks that disrupt the everyday activate audience engagement with urban environments”. Here, Pam invites new spatial practices of detours, documentation and digital sharing, transforming hidden corners into social spaces of discovery.

Situated within a fenced-off construction zone, the artwork is partly hidden from passing train passengers, transforming it into a secret gem. The mural’s vivid colours sharply contrast with the surrounding concrete and scaffolding, exemplifying how street art can challenge the monotony of urban infrastructure while asserting a quiet, defiant claim on public space.
