Stop 4

This mural, painted across the large silos of the former Uncle Toby’s factory reclaims and reinterprets Melbourne’s industrial heritage. Silos hold cultural significance across Australia, once standing as symbols of agricultural productivity, strategically built along railway lines. With the decline of rail transport, many fell into disuse, later reimagined through muralism as agents of renewal and identity (Potter & Johanson, 2023). Positioned on a surface once dominated by corporate branding, Uncle Toby’s silo mural draws on this broader tradition of creative reuse, but within an urban context, transforming this site of consumption into one of expression and community memory (Gunn, 2020).

Its monumental scale and visibility from passing trains reinforce what Ferrel and Weide (2020) describe as spot theory: the pursuit of high-risk, high-visibility locations that elevate artistic prestige. By situating the work in such a monumental yet hazardous zone, the artist transforms a fleeting commuter view into a public gesture and a mark of artistic recognition.
