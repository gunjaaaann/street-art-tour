Stop 1

Through 6 key stops, this tour examines how Pam the Bird transforms industrial remnants and transport infrastructure into sites of visual engagement, risk, and collective identity.

Positioned in hard to reach, often transient spaces, Pam reflects what Dovey, Wollan and Woodcock (2012) describe as the spatial logic of graffiti – an ongoing negotiation between visibility, risk and belonging. Along the Sunbury train line where walls, fences and silos form the visual margins of the city, Pam transforms these neglected infrastructures into active participants in Melbourne’s cultural geography. Seen fleetingly by commuters, Pam occupies a paradoxical position - simultaneously hidden and highly public.

Graffiti scholarship often highlights the relationship between visibility, danger and social hierarchy. Ferrell and Weide (2010) argue that status is conferred upon those who access highly exposed, risky locations, demonstrating that graffiti operates within a controlled spatial logic rather than anarchic disorder. This theoretical framework is relevant when analysing Pam the bird along the Sunbury train line between Flinders Street and Southern Cross Station effectively illustrating how artistic presence in liminal transit spaces negotiates both the aesthetic and infrastructural dimensions of the city.

Situated on a fence against an industrial wall, Pam’s visibility is fleeting, legible only to commuters in motion. The artwork’s placement—visible only from passing trains—creates a sense of exclusivity and transience, as if it exists purely for those in motion. Through the deliberate positioning of Pam in a perilous and transient urban space, the artist elevates the work from a fleeting sight into an intentional public statement, where danger operates as a conduit of artistic recognition.
